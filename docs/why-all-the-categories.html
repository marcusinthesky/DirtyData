Why all the categories! 🙀

Published Mon 02 July 2018 in posts

by Marcus Gawronsky categorical

Whether we like it or not the world is filled with categorical data! While categorical data seems the go to tool for surveys authors, categories can be a nightmare for Data Scientists looking to model and understand relationships in the data. Many datasets contain vague categories, categories which can appear non-sensically similar and imbalanced- and this is a serious problem we try to model this data. 🔺⚫🔶🔺🔴

Vague and similar labels can result in a lot of correlation between categories, but also can render survey data ambiguous and participants rush to fill things out. When we have lot of categories for a specific variable this can increase the dimensionality of our data, making both the estimation and the significance of variables challenging. Small categories with one observation can really take on any value, preventing our model from generalizing or creating a skewed R-square, AIC or losses. What do we do! 🙏

What you immediately realize... There are a lot of categories and alot of them don't really make sense or are just way too small. There is only 1 mansion. Are Duplex O/S and Duplex Cottage that different? idk. 😶 But a cool technique to answer this question is Correspondance Analysis (CA). 🔗 So I'm not going to get into too much of the maths, as I will let my messy code speak for itself, but what we do is we calculate the some 'standardized' difference between our expected frequencies- based on their column and row counts- and we then use Princple Component Analysis (PCA) on it. 📐 It's cool. 😎

In [9]:
expected = pd.DataFrame(data=None, index=observed.index.tolist(),
                        columns=observed.columns.tolist())

for i in range(0,observed.shape[1]):
    for j in range(0,observed.shape[0]):
        expected.iloc[j,i] = observed.apply(sum, axis=0)[i] * \
        observed.apply(sum, axis=1)[j] / sum(observed.apply(sum, axis=1))
In [10]:
pearson = (observed - expected ) / expected**0.5
scaled = pearson / sum(observed.apply(sum, axis=1))**0.5
In [11]:
u, s, v = np.linalg.svd(scaled.values.astype('float'), 
                        full_matrices=False, compute_uv=True)
